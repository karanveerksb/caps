---

**Target Venue:** IEEE Access  
**Submission Timeline:** 6 Months  
**Date:** March 2026

## 1. Overview

Deepfake detection has advanced rapidly, with modern audio-visual models achieving high classification accuracy on standard benchmarks. However, a critical and largely unsolved problem remains: the explanations produced by these detectors are not verified to be faithful. A detector can claim "this video is fake because of the audio" while internally having based that decision on the visual stream. This gap between what an explanation claims and what the model actually computed is known as the **faithfulness problem** in Explainable AI (XAI).

This project proposes the first framework to formally evaluate the faithfulness of audio-visual deepfake explanations using per-modality ground-truth manipulation labels. The central contribution is the **Cross-Modal Attribution Score (CMAS)** — a novel metric that uses cosine similarity between the model's attribution vector and the ground-truth manipulation vector derived from FakeAVCeleb's per-modality category labels. This evaluation is made possible by a property of FakeAVCeleb that no prior paper has exploited for XAI validation: the dataset explicitly labels whether the audio, the video, or both were manipulated in each sample.

## 2. Problem Statement

### 2.1 The Deepfake Threat

Synthetic media generated by deep learning poses a serious and growing threat to information integrity. Manipulated videos have been used to spread political disinformation, fabricate non-consensual content, and commit financial fraud. Automated detection systems are now essential infrastructure for social media platforms, journalism, and legal proceedings.

### 2.2 The Accuracy–Interpretability Gap

Existing deepfake detectors are black boxes. They produce a binary prediction (real or fake) with a confidence score, but provide no insight into which signal — the face, the voice, or a specific temporal moment — drove the decision. This renders them practically unusable in high-stakes forensic contexts. A court cannot act on "the model says 93% fake." It needs to know why.

### 2.3 The Faithfulness Problem — The Core Gap

Several recent papers have added explanation modules to deepfake detectors. However, none have verified that these explanations are **faithful** — that the explanation actually reflects the model's internal computation rather than producing a plausible-looking but inaccurate justification.

The faithfulness problem in multimodal deepfake detection has three concrete layers:

1. **Modality attribution faithfulness:** When the model attributes its decision to audio manipulation, was the audio stream actually the dominant signal in the internal computation?
2. **Spatial faithfulness:** When a visual heatmap highlights a specific facial region, does removing that region actually change the model's prediction?
3. **Cross-modal consistency:** Are the visual and audio attribution maps internally consistent with each other, given what is known about which modality was forged?

No prior work addresses all three in a multimodal deepfake setting with ground-truth verification. This project does.

## 3. Novelty — Why This Gets Published

### 3.1 What Already Exists (Verified Against Literature, March 2026)

| Area | What Exists | Gap |
|---|---|---|
| Multimodal deepfake detection | AVMCD (2025), AVT²-DWF (IEEE SPL 2024), MSOC (2024) — high accuracy, dynamic modality weighting | None report explanation faithfulness |
| XAI for deepfake detection | GradCAM/SHAP on visual-only detectors; natural language explanations (ACM MM 2025) | All unimodal or unverified |
| Faithfulness evaluation | PGI/PGU for image classifiers; M4 benchmark for image+text | Never applied to audio-visual deepfake pipelines |
| MSOC modality attribution | Architectural multi-stream approach that outputs which modality is more likely fake | Uses structural separation, not post-hoc XAI on a fused model |

### 3.2 The Precise Gap This Project Fills

No prior paper has used FakeAVCeleb's per-modality ground-truth labels (FARV, RAFV, FAFV) to evaluate whether **post-hoc attribution methods** correctly identify the manipulated modality in a **cross-attention fused model**. MSOC builds separate branches and reports modality scores by design — it is an architectural choice, not a faithfulness evaluation. The CMAS metric introduced here is the first external, quantitative test of post-hoc XAI faithfulness for multimodal deepfake detection.

### 3.3 Four Original Contributions

**Contribution 1 — Audio-visual detection framework.**  
A cross-modal transformer fusion architecture that jointly processes facial features (EfficientNet-B4) and audio features (wav2vec 2.0) using bidirectional cross-attention to model inter-modal inconsistencies.

**Contribution 2 — Per-modality attribution modules.**  
Independent explanation modules for each stream: GradCAM for the visual pathway, Integrated Gradients for the audio pathway. These produce separate attribution maps that can be compared and evaluated independently.

**Contribution 3 — Cross-Modal Attribution Score (CMAS). [Primary novel contribution]**  
A faithfulness metric defined as the cosine similarity between the model's normalized attribution vector A = [v, a] and the ground-truth manipulation vector G derived from FakeAVCeleb category labels. This is the first faithfulness metric in deepfake XAI to exploit per-modality dataset labels as an objective oracle.

**Contribution 4 — Multimodal faithfulness evaluation protocol.**  
A reproducible protocol adapting PGI and PGU to the audio-visual setting, enabling joint faithfulness evaluation across both modalities simultaneously. This can serve as a standard benchmark for future multimodal XAI research in deepfake detection.

### 3.4 What This Work Does NOT Claim

To maintain a rigorous and defensible scope, this project explicitly does not claim:

- State-of-the-art detection accuracy (accuracy is a secondary metric)
- Zero-shot generalization to unseen deepfake methods
- Temporal localization of manipulated segments
- Robustness under video compression or noise perturbations
- Text or sentiment modality integration
- Natural language explanation generation

These are valid future extensions but are out of scope for a 6-month publication target.

## 4. Related Work

### 4.1 Multimodal Deepfake Detection

Early deepfake detectors operated on single modalities — typically the visual stream. Audio-visual fusion methods emerged around 2022, with frameworks jointly modeling lip sync, vocal prosody, and facial motion. FakeAVCeleb (NeurIPS 2021) established the benchmark for multimodal detection, providing labeled videos across four manipulation categories. Recent methods such as AVMCD (2025) and AVT²-DWF (IEEE Signal Processing Letters, 2024) have pushed accuracy above 96% on FakeAVCeleb using dynamic modality weighting and cross-modal consistency verification.

**Gap:** These methods focus entirely on detection performance. Explanation quality is unreported and faithfulness is never evaluated.

### 4.2 Explainable AI for Deepfake Detection

Several works have introduced visual explanations for deepfake detectors, primarily using GradCAM, SHAP, or saliency maps on image-based detectors. A 2025 systematic review categorized these into forensic analysis methods, post-hoc model-centric methods, and natural language generation approaches. DeepfakeJudge (CVPR 2026) uses an MLLM-as-a-judge approach to evaluate visual reasoning fidelity. TriDF (2025) compares model-identified artifacts against human-annotated evidence.

**Gap:** All existing XAI approaches for deepfake detection are either unimodal (visual only) or produce explanations without quantitative faithfulness evaluation against ground truth. Critically, none evaluate whether explanations correctly identify the manipulated modality in audio-visual settings.

### 4.3 Faithfulness Evaluation in XAI

The general XAI literature has developed metrics for faithfulness evaluation. PGI (Prediction Gap on Important features) measures how much accuracy drops when the most important features are removed. PGU (Prediction Gap on Unimportant features) measures the converse. The M4 benchmark (NeurIPS 2023) established faithfulness evaluation for image and text classifiers, explicitly noting that future work should extend to audio and multimodal settings.

**Gap:** No faithfulness evaluation methodology exists for multimodal audio-visual deepfake detectors. Adapting PGI/PGU to the audio-visual setting and combining them with ground-truth modality labels (CMAS) is the primary technical novelty of this project — directly answering the open problem identified by M4.

## 5. Methodology

### 5.1 Dataset

**Primary dataset: FakeAVCeleb**

FakeAVCeleb contains approximately 20,000 videos across four categories:

| Category | Binary Label | Meaning | Role in This Project |
|---|---|---|---|
| RARV | Real | Real video, real audio | Negative class |
| FARV | Fake | Fake video, real audio | Fake — visual manipulation ground truth |
| RAFV | Fake | Real video, fake audio | Fake — audio manipulation ground truth |
| FAFV | Fake | Fake video, fake audio | Fake — both manipulated |

The FARV and RAFV categories are the critical ones. They provide ground truth for CMAS evaluation: in FARV videos the visual attribution should dominate; in RAFV videos the audio attribution should dominate. FAFV videos are used as a sensitivity test, excluded from the primary CMAS calculation.

**Split:** 70% train / 10% validation / 20% test, stratified by category.

**Secondary dataset: DFDC (test only)**  
Used exclusively for cross-dataset generalization AUC reporting. Not used for CMAS evaluation — DFDC lacks per-modality labels.

### 5.2 System Architecture

The system has five components:

```
Input Video
    |
    |--- Visual stream: Face crops (224x224, 1fps, MediaPipe detection)
    |         |
    |         EfficientNet-B4 (ImageNet pretrained)
    |         |
    |         F_v : 1792-dim visual feature vector
    |
    |--- Audio stream: Raw waveform (16kHz mono)
              |
              wav2vec 2.0 (LibriSpeech pretrained)
              |
              F_a : pooled audio feature vector
                   |
         Cross-Modal Fusion Transformer
         (bidirectional cross-attention: F_v queries F_a, then F_a queries F_v)
                   |
            Binary classifier (2-layer MLP, dropout=0.3)
                   |
              Real / Fake prediction
                   |
      |------------|------------|
      |                         |
  GradCAM on F_v          Integrated Gradients on F_a
  (spatial heatmap A_v)   (temporal attribution A_a)
      |                         |
      |---------- CMAS ----------|
      cosine_similarity([norm(A_v), norm(A_a)], G)
      PGI_v, PGU_v, PGI_a, PGU_a
```

**Component A — Visual encoder.**  
EfficientNet-B4 pretrained on ImageNet (via timm). Face regions extracted using MediaPipe FaceDetection with 20% bounding box expansion. Input shape: `[T, 3, 224, 224]` (T frames at 1fps). Temporal aggregation: mean pooling across frames. Output: 1792-dimensional vector F_v.

Why EfficientNet-B4 and not alternatives:
- ResNet-50: Older architecture, lower accuracy per parameter, weaker at fine-grained texture artifacts
- ViT-B/16: Requires significantly more data to generalize; FakeAVCeleb at 20k videos is insufficient for stable ViT training from scratch
- EfficientNet-B4: Best accuracy/compute trade-off for face artifact detection, extensively validated in deepfake literature

**Component B — Audio encoder.**  
wav2vec 2.0 base model pretrained on LibriSpeech 960h (via HuggingFace Transformers). Input: raw waveform at 16kHz. Output: frame-level representations mean-pooled to fixed-length vector F_a.

Why wav2vec 2.0 and not alternatives:
- MFCC + CNN: Handcrafted features miss subtle neural synthesis artifacts that wav2vec 2.0 detects via learned self-supervised representations
- HuBERT: Marginally stronger but significantly heavier; wav2vec 2.0 base is sufficient and faster to fine-tune
- Whisper encoder: Designed for ASR, not trained to detect synthesis artifacts

**Component C — Cross-modal fusion transformer.**  
A two-layer transformer encoder with bidirectional cross-attention. Layer 1: F_v is query, F_a is key/value. Layer 2: F_a is query, F_v is key/value. This forces the model to learn bidirectional inter-modal dependencies and is critical for detecting single-modality manipulations.

Why cross-attention and not simple concatenation:  
Concatenation treats both modalities as equal independent inputs and ignores their interaction. Cross-attention explicitly models whether the face features and audio features are consistent with each other — which is precisely the signal that single-modality deepfakes (FARV, RAFV) disrupt.

**Component D — Binary classifier.**  
Two-layer MLP with GELU activation and dropout (p=0.3) applied to the fused representation. Output: probability of being fake.

**Component E — Attribution modules.**  
- *Visual:* GradCAM applied to the last convolutional layer of EfficientNet-B4. Produces spatial heatmap A_v ∈ R^(H×W) per frame, averaged across T frames. Implementation: Captum library.
- *Audio:* Integrated Gradients applied to wav2vec 2.0 output representations with silence baseline. Produces temporal attribution A_a ∈ R^(T_a) over audio frames. Implementation: Captum library.

Why GradCAM for visual and not SHAP:  
SHAP with KernelExplainer on video frames is computationally prohibitive (hours per sample at video resolution). GradCAM runs in milliseconds and operates directly on the model's gradient flow.

Why Integrated Gradients for audio and not GradCAM:  
GradCAM is designed for convolutional layers with spatial structure. wav2vec 2.0 uses transformer self-attention — there are no convolutional feature maps to localize. Integrated Gradients operates on any differentiable model and satisfies the completeness and sensitivity axioms, making it theoretically grounded for attribution on transformer outputs.

### 5.3 The CMAS Metric — Formal Definition

Let a post-hoc XAI method produce attribution scores for each modality. Define the normalized attribution vector as:

```
A = [v_score, a_score]

where:
  v_score = sum of all values in A_v (visual attribution map)
  a_score = sum of all values in A_a (audio attribution map)
  A is L1-normalized so v_score + a_score = 1
```

Define the ground-truth manipulation vector G from FakeAVCeleb category labels:

```
FARV (fake video, real audio)  → G = [1, 0]
RAFV (real video, fake audio)  → G = [0, 1]
FAFV (both fake)               → G = [0.5, 0.5]  (sensitivity test only)
```

The CMAS for a single video is the cosine similarity between A and G:

```
CMAS_i = (A · G) / (||A|| × ||G||)
```

The aggregate CMAS over the test set is the mean over all N fake videos with single-modality ground truth (FARV + RAFV only):

```
CMAS = (1/N) × Σ CMAS_i
```

**Interpretation:**
- CMAS = 1.0 → perfect faithfulness; the explanation always identified the correct manipulated modality
- CMAS = 0.707 → attribution vector at 45 degrees to ground truth; moderate faithfulness
- CMAS = 0.5 → random baseline (attributing 50/50 to both modalities regardless of ground truth)
- CMAS < 0.5 → systematically misattributing to the wrong modality

A publishable result requires CMAS significantly above 0.5 (random), target ≥ 0.75.

This cosine similarity formulation is more mathematically principled than a binary dominance vote: it rewards partial correctness and allows gradient comparison between XAI methods and across model configurations.

### 5.4 PGI and PGU Adaptation to Multimodal Setting

Standard PGI removes the top-k most important features and measures accuracy drop. Standard PGU removes the least important features and expects minimal accuracy change. We adapt both to operate independently on each modality stream:

**PGI_v:** Zero out the top-20% highest-valued pixels in A_v across all frames. Re-run inference. Report AUC drop.

**PGI_a:** Zero out the top-20% highest-attributed audio frames ranked by A_a. Re-run inference. Report AUC drop.

**PGU_v:** Zero out the bottom-20% lowest-valued pixels in A_v. Re-run inference. Report AUC change (expected: minimal).

**PGU_a:** Zero out the bottom-20% lowest-attributed audio frames. Re-run inference. Report AUC change (expected: minimal).

A faithful explanation has high PGI (accuracy drops substantially when important features are removed) and low PGU (accuracy is stable when unimportant features are removed). These metrics validate CMAS from a different angle: CMAS measures modality-level correctness; PGI/PGU measure feature-level causal importance.

### 5.5 Training Details

| Hyperparameter | Value | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Optimizer | AdamW | Better weight decay regularization than Adam for transformer components |
| Learning rate — encoders | 1e-4 | Lower LR to avoid destroying pretrained representations |
| Learning rate — classifier/fusion | 1e-3 | Higher LR for randomly initialized components |
| Batch size | 32 | Fits H100 memory with full-precision training |
| Epochs | 30 with early stopping (patience=5) | Prevents overfitting to FakeAVCeleb manipulation artifacts |
| Loss | Binary cross-entropy | Standard for binary detection |
| GPU | NVIDIA H100 | Available |
| Framework | PyTorch 2.2 | Current stable release |
| EfficientNet weights | ImageNet pretrained (timm library) | Standard initialization |
| wav2vec 2.0 weights | LibriSpeech 960h pretrained (HuggingFace) | Self-supervised speech representations |
| Dropout | p=0.3 in MLP classifier | Regularization against dataset-specific shortcuts |
| Preprocessing — visual | 1fps, MediaPipe face detection, 20% bbox expansion, 224×224, ImageNet normalization | Standard for face-based deepfake detection |
| Preprocessing — audio | 16kHz mono, raw waveform for wav2vec 2.0 | As per wav2vec 2.0 input specification |

## 6. Evaluation Plan

### 6.1 Detection Performance Metrics

| Metric | Purpose |
|---|---|
| AUC-ROC | Primary detection performance; threshold-independent |

**Targets:** AUC ≥ 0.93 on FakeAVCeleb test set; AUC ≥ 0.78 on DFDC (cross-dataset, no training).

### 6.4 Cross-Dataset Generalization

Train exclusively on FakeAVCeleb (70% split). Test on DFDC. Report AUC only — DFDC has no per-modality labels so CMAS cannot be computed. Purpose: demonstrate the model is not overfit to FakeAVCeleb-specific artifacts.

### 6.5 Failure Case Analysis

For videos where CMAS is low (explanation incorrectly attributed to wrong modality), conduct qualitative analysis of:
- Whether the detection was still correct (high accuracy but wrong explanation)
- Whether FAFV videos (both modalities fake) show systematic attribution patterns
- Whether specific manipulation types in FakeAVCeleb yield systematically lower CMAS

This analysis becomes Section IV-D (Discussion) in the paper and demonstrates scientific rigor beyond simple metric reporting.

## 8. Expected Outcomes

### 8.1 Primary Result

A CMAS score significantly above 0.5 (random baseline), demonstrating that Integrated Gradients on the audio stream correctly identifies audio-manipulated videos (RAFV) and GradCAM on the visual stream correctly identifies visually-manipulated videos (FARV). Target: CMAS ≥ 0.75. Statistical significance reported via 5-run averaging and confidence intervals.

### 8.2 Secondary Results

- Competitive AUC on FakeAVCeleb test set (target ≥ 0.93)
- Non-trivial AUC on DFDC (target ≥ 0.78), demonstrating generalization
- PGI_v and PGI_a showing significant AUC drops when top-20% attributed features are removed, confirming causal link between attribution maps and detection decisions
- Ablation study confirming that cross-attention fusion outperforms simple concatenation on both AUC and CMAS

### 8.3 Paper Outcome

One IEEE Access publication. The paper's main claim — "first quantitative faithfulness evaluation of audio-visual deepfake explanation attribution using per-modality ground truth" — is narrow, verifiable, and uncontested in the current literature as of March 2026.

## 11. Conclusion

This proposal presents a focused, novel, and feasible research project targeting IEEE Access publication within six months. The work addresses a genuine and documented gap in the literature: the complete absence of faithfulness evaluation for multimodal deepfake explanations using ground-truth modality labels.

The proposed Cross-Modal Attribution Score (CMAS) is a concrete, measurable, and verifiable contribution enabled by the unique structure of the FakeAVCeleb dataset — a property that has been available since 2021 but never exploited for XAI validation. The scope is deliberately narrow: this project does not claim the best detector, only the first honestly evaluated explainer. This is precisely the kind of precise, defensible, single-contribution paper that IEEE Access reviewers accept.

The architecture is purpose-built for this evaluation: EfficientNet-B4 and wav2vec 2.0 are the most validated encoders for their respective modalities in deepfake detection; cross-attention fusion is the correct mechanism for modeling inter-modal inconsistency; GradCAM and Integrated Gradients are the computationally feasible and theoretically grounded attribution methods for convolutional and transformer architectures respectively. Every choice is justified, every alternative is documented, and every claim is bounded.
